    vtol-uav

    VTOL UAV is this year's attempt at the Aerial Evolution Association of Canada's 2023 student competition. The mission is similar to last year's, with an increased focus on high maneuverability in urban environments. Drawing on our lessons from the previous year's competition, as well as observations on the War in Ukraine, our team has decided on creating a hybrid drone, a vertical take-off and landing unmanned aerial system (VTOL UAV).

    The main reasons for the move are the fact that a high endurance platform is needed, and any platform that relies on rotars to provide lift will be inherently less efficient as the energy spent on creating forward momentum does not contribute to lift like it does for an aircraft with wings. The VTOL ability is required for the urban air maneuverability aspect. A lesson retained from the previous year is that while a hexarotor design does provide excellent lift and reliability, its design is heavy and hinders the range and agility of the platform. Switching to a VTOL UAV will allow us to move large distances fast, and small distances with agility.

    Switching to this design has not been easy, as there are many design challenges that come with VTOLs. Before that, we first needed to prove we can design an aircraft, and understand the dynamics and manufacturing challenges. Given that our team heavily prioritizes flight testing in realistic conditions, it would be inevitable that we would burn through a lot of airframes. Thus, creating a wing in a traditional process like using balsa and monocoat would require too much time to be practical.

    Instead we opted to 3D print a wing using lightweight PLA. Specific challenges arose from the need to create supports for the wing while ensuring a smooth continuous path for the printer nozzle. We were able to 3D print an aerofoil with the profile of the NACA 4412 that should be able to sustain a load factor of 3, with the ability to scale up this number should we decide to create an air dominance platform instead of an air taxi. However, due to the nature of laminated layers, we cannot accurately simulate how the system will behave. Luckily it is dirt cheap and simple to create these wings.

    The challenge now shifts to creating the rest of the airframe, implementing control surfaces, installing onboard electronics for the onboard flight computer, C2 link, and payload. This doesn't scratch the surface on the software challenges that we'll face, from creating a model to represent our dynamic system, tuning said system, and then integrating the autonomous flight system.

    I'm excited to see what's in store for me and my team, and the sort of challenges we will face and overcome. No one's ever changed the world overnight, but we have a garage, a soldering iron, and many laptops with GPUs! As the winter approaches, I can't help but think that my biggest challenge will be figuring out how to stay warm when flight testing, and make sure none of the electronics exceed their operating temperatures....
